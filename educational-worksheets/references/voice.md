# Worksheet Voice — universal principles + English calibration

Load this for the **mandatory microcopy pass** (SKILL.md → "Worksheet voice — the prose contract"). Rewrite every student-facing line — instructions, framing, prompts — against it.

Two layers: **universal prose principles** apply in any language; the **English calibration corpus** (slop-vs-good pairs, positive specimens) is English-only — for another language, hold the principles and judge against good writing in *that* language rather than translating the pairs.

The throughline, in any language: **a line can read brisk and confident and still be slop.** Tone is not the test. The test is — can a student act on it, is every word concrete, is it a full grammatical sentence?

---

## Universal prose principles (any language)

### Instruction vs. framing — two categories, written differently

These are not the same kind of line, and the difference is load-bearing:

- An **instruction** tells the student to *do* something. It opens on a plain imperative verb with **only that verb bolded** (**Write**, **Circle**, **Explain**, **Find**), then states the whole action as a full sentence. When one instruction issues two coordinated commands (joined by *and* or *then*), bold **each** command verb (**Choose** the line… and **write down** what makes it read as an order) — but bold only verbs that directly command the student, never a verb inside an explanation, an example, or a subordinate clause.
- A **framing line** tells the student what a section is *for*. It is a plain full sentence or a genuine question — **not bolded, not an imperative**. In the markup it is the `.section-framing` paragraph under the header.
- The category is the prose form, not the CSS slot: a framing line orients (purpose/question), an instruction directs (bolded verb). When a section opens by directing an action — the "now do it yourself" line at the blank stage of a worked→guided→blank progression — that opener is an *instruction* with a bolded verb, not a framing line. Keep them apart: never bold a framing line into a fake command, and never write an instruction as a vague noun-phrase ("How I can tell them apart:").

### The pass, distilled

Run every student-facing line through these (the bracketed examples are English illustrations; each rule itself is language-universal):

1. **Verb-first instruction.** An instruction opens on a bolded plain imperative and states the whole action — bold each verb when it issues two coordinated commands (**Choose**… and **write**…) — no noun-phrase prompts ("How I…", "What the…"), no figurative verb dressing a plain action ("hunt", "dig into", "unpack").
2. **Actable.** Every verb is a concrete action and every object is named. If a student could ask "do what, exactly?", rewrite. No "picking", "start your piece", "see it", "it marks a spot".
3. **Full sentence.** Subject + finite verb. Fragments only for genuine labels ("**Genre:** review").
4. **Grammatical.** Would a colleague mark it? Fix slips like "write perfect" (adjective for adverb) or "enough that the piece exists".
5. **Plain.** No coined label to decode ("the engine", "the move", "the verdict"), no abstraction for a concrete thing ("the surface of a text"), no motion/arrival metaphor ("the meaning lands"), no contrast-negation frame ("it's X, not Y" in English) — state the point directly. No filler, no generic praise, no manufactured stakes.
6. **Trusting.** On an open or creative task, give the prompt and the goal — not the method, when choosing *how* is the skill being practised.
7. **Student is the subject.** Write to them ("You are asked to include one of your own ideas"), not about the task ("The essay asks for…").
8. **Colour-independent.** No instruction that breaks on a grayscale photocopy ("underline in blue").
9. **Names the task, not the furniture.** State the task and the form of the answer; never name the generic writing apparatus the student can already see — no "on the lines", "in the box", "in the space", "on the grid", "use the dotted area". A visible line, grid, or box already says *write here*. **Exception:** naming a structure that IS the academic concept — a number line, a coordinate grid, a Venn diagram, a timeline, a table — is fine; that is content, not furniture.

### Then read it cold — three checks the per-line pass misses

The pass above runs line by line. These three only surface when you read the **assembled** sheet as a stranger would — a student meeting only this page, in order — so make one fresh pass over the whole thing once it is built:

- **No header echo.** The first body line under a header must add something the header didn't. Flag any opener that merely restates its title ("Week 2 — Letter / email" → "This week's genre is the letter / email."), and any kicker that only repeats the adjacent heading.
- **Each section stands on its own.** A student reading one section in isolation must not be sent to something named only in an earlier one ("take it apart through the same five lenses" — *which* five lenses?). Name the thing, or cite where it was introduced ("the five lenses on page 2").
- **Every referent is named.** Flag a loose *this / it / that / these / them* whose antecedent is far, missing, or ambiguous by the point the reader reaches it, and rewrite to name the thing ("…but you will use *it* whenever you ask for anything in writing" → "…but you will soften a request whenever you ask for anything in writing"). Check within a passage, not only across sections — it is a common AI tell.

## English calibration corpus (English only)

**English filler to cut:** *explore, unlock, journey, delve, dive in, foster, empower, critical thinking* used as filler; the "it's X, not Y" contrast frame; generic praise ("Great job!"); manufactured stakes ("master this or fall behind").

### Slop → good

#### Instructions

**Open every instruction on a plain, bolded imperative verb — a complete command.**
- ❌ "How I can tell them apart:" · "Hunt the on-the-nose lines."
- ✅ "**Explain** how you can tell the two speakers apart." · "**Find** the lines where a character says exactly what they feel."
- Tell: a noun-phrase prompt ("How I…", "What the…") makes the student infer the task; a figurative verb ("hunt", "dig into", "unpack") dresses a plain action. Name the action; bold only the verb.

**Name the concrete action and effect — no vague verbs.**
- ❌ "You're picking your creative piece today and starting it." · "Circle each one: it marks a spot where you summarised."
- ✅ "Choose your creative piece today and write your opening." · "Circle each telling word — that's where you summarised instead of showed."
- Tell: "picking", "starting it", "it marks a spot" name nothing a student can do. If they could ask "do what, exactly?", rewrite.

**Don't pre-pick the method on an open or creative task — let them choose how.**
- ❌ "A janitor on a spaceship. Use one action and one object they touch."
- ✅ "A janitor on a spaceship." (the prompt and the goal — choosing *how* to show it is the skill being practised)
- Tell: the nudge does the interesting part for the student. If the lesson taught the method, naming which one to use removes the practice.

**Don't make an instruction depend on colour.**
- ❌ "Underline each tool in the colour shown below."
- ✅ "Underline each tool, and label which one it is."
- Tell: a black-and-white photocopy erases the colour, and the instruction breaks.

**Name the task, not the writing surface.**
- ❌ "Work it out on the grid, then write your answer in the box." · "Fill the space with words, anywhere, any size." · "Use the dotted area to sketch the rectangle."
- ✅ "Work out how many coaches are needed." · "Write every word you connect with the sea." · "Sketch the rectangle and find its area."
- Tell: the student can see the grid, box, or field — naming it adds words and reads as talking down. State what to do and what form the answer takes; the visible space carries the rest. The exception is a named academic structure (number line, coordinate grid, Venn diagram, timeline, table) — that's the concept, not furniture, and naming it is right.

#### Framing & explanation

**Full sentences, grammatically correct — no fragments, no colloquial slips.**
- ❌ "Don't try to write perfect — write enough that the piece exists." · "A one-line sketch each — compression is the point."
- ✅ "Don't aim for perfect — just get enough words down that you have something to work with." · "Write one sentence for each idea; you'll expand the one you choose."
- Tell: "write perfect" (adjective for adverb), "the piece exists" / "compression is the point" (abstract aphorism), and verbless fragments read brisk but model sloppy writing in a writing class.

**Plain words, not coined labels or fancy nouns.**
- ❌ "Step 2 — The engine: purposeful detail." · "the move" · "the flat versions tell you the verdict."
- ✅ "Step 2 — Telling words, and what to do instead."
- Tell: "the engine / the move / the verdict" make the reader decode a label before they can act. Just name the thing.

**Concrete nouns, not abstractions.**
- ❌ "AI may help with the surface of a text you wrote."
- ✅ "AI can help tidy the spelling and grammar of a text you wrote."

**Plain verbs, not motion or arrival metaphors.**
- ❌ "The meaning lands." · "Said and asked … vanish quietly."
- ✅ "The meaning comes through." · "Most of the time, said and asked are all you need, and a reader hardly notices them."

**State the point directly — drop the "it's X, not Y" frame.**
- ❌ "It's a useful instinct, not a law: an adverb that adds something stays."
- ✅ "Cutting -ly adverbs is worth doing — but keep one that genuinely adds something."

**Name the action and its object — don't compress it into an idiom.**
- ❌ "…make their stance visible early and close on it."
- ✅ "…make their stance visible early and restate it in the conclusion."

**The student is the subject, not the course object.**
- ❌ "The essay asks for one idea of your own."
- ✅ "You are asked to include one of your own ideas."

### Positive specimens — the calibration target

This is what good worksheet prose reads like in English. Match the register, not the content.

- "Every writer leans on a few favourite words — *very, really, just, suddenly.* Once is fine; by the third time, the reader notices."
- "Read one page of your draft aloud and break up every sentence you can't comfortably read in one breath."
- "**Describe** how the two speakers sound different — point to their word choice, their sentence rhythm, their attitude." · "**Work out** what each of them actually wants in this conversation, and how the two wants pull against each other." · "**Explain** how the narrator talks about the two of them, and what that adds on top of the dialogue."

Each is a full grammatical sentence, opens on a plain verb where it's an instruction, names concrete actions, and carries warmth in word choice rather than in filler or fragments.
